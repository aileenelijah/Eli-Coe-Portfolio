Section I
Original
Concepts

Four campaigns. One point of view. Each started with a specific brand problem and a deliberate choice about the emotional territory no one else was claiming.

Concept I

The Wave.

For resilience-first consumer brands that talk about strength but only ever show the triumphant after, never the difficult middle.

The Problem

Resilience advertising has a specific failure mode: it shows the breakthrough. It markets to the winner while ignoring the enormous, underserved audience still in it — not broken, not done, just still going. Underrepresented in every category that talks about strength, because those campaigns are built around the after.

The Campaign
You’ve been knocked down enough times to stop counting.
Point at the next one.
Executions
Billboard

The wave is coming anyway.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Short

You stopped asking if you could handle it. You already know you can.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Medium

There’s a specific kind of person who has been through enough that fear stopped being the deciding factor. They don’t feel brave. They just stopped waiting to.  [Brand]

Video VO — 60s

You’ve been knocked down by enough of them to know what they feel like coming. The fear doesn’t go away. You just stop letting it decide. Point at the next one. [Brand]. For the ones still in it.

⎯⎯⎯
Concept II

Runway.

For creator tools, AI products, and platforms built around the gap between the idea and the moment it actually gets made.

The Problem

Most ideas don’t make it — for lack of runway, not capability, not vision. The filmmaker who knew exactly what the shot was supposed to look like. The writer who had the story for three years. They didn’t need more talent. They needed somewhere for the idea to go.

The Campaign
You finally have the runway.
Make the thing you’ve been carrying.
Executions
Billboard

The idea was never the problem. You just didn’t have the runway.  [Brand]

OOH

The fist opens.  [Brand]

Video VO

You’ve had this idea for a long time. You knew exactly what it was supposed to look like. You just didn’t have the runway to make it real. The brain is limited by time. A fist slowly closing. You finally have the runway. Make the thing you’ve been carrying. [Brand]

Concept III

Time.

For brands where survival mode is the real competitor — the thing consuming the customer’s time without their consent.

The Problem

Time is the only thing that doesn’t come back. The particular cost of being in survival mode is that it consumes time without consent. The brands that name the specific grief of time that slipped away earn trust that brands who only talk about the future never reach.

The Campaign
You can’t get it back.
You can decide what you do with what’s left.
Executions
Billboard

Later keeps becoming now.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Short

You’re not running out of time. You’re running out of laters.  [Brand]

Video VO — 30s

You’ve been telling yourself later. Later has a way of becoming now without asking permission. You already know what you’ve been waiting to do. [Brand]

Concept IV

Do It Anyway.

For high-friction categories where the customer has been circling the decision for months and hasn’t moved.

The Problem

Most brands in high-friction categories try to reduce perceived risk. Copy written for someone who just needed a small push. It doesn’t reach the person who has been thinking about this for a year, who doesn’t feel ready, who has failed before. Easier isn’t the answer. Permission to move without feeling ready is.

The Campaign
You’re not ready. You never were.
Do it anyway.
Executions
Billboard

You’ve survived everything so far.  [Brand]

Paid Social

Nobody feels ready. The people who do the thing anyway just stopped waiting to.  [Brand]

Email Subject

You’ve been waiting to feel ready.

Video VO

You didn’t feel ready for any of this. You did it anyway. The only kind of ready that exists. [Brand]

Section III
Diagnostic
Work

Copy that doesn’t work usually has a problem underneath the problem. The diagnosis comes before the copy — because if you don’t identify what’s actually wrong, the rewrite just covers it up.

I — Feature-heavy product messaging

Copy describes what the product does without connecting to what the user wants. Positioned against competitors instead of against the user’s existing frustration.

Before
Our platform provides innovative solutions to streamline your workflow and enhance productivity outcomes.
After
You already know what’s slowing you down. We help you fix it — without adding three more tools to your stack.
The original speaks about the product. The rewrite speaks to the experience — and pre-empts the tool-fatigue objection in the same breath.

II — Luxury brand over-explaining

Copy tries to justify the price point instead of assuming the customer already understands value. The moment you explain luxury, the positioning battle is already lost.

Before
Our products are crafted using the highest quality ingredients, carefully sourced from around the world.
After
If you know, you know.
The original is written for someone who needs convincing. The rewrite is written for someone who doesn’t. Restraint is the signal.

III — Transactional lifecycle messaging

Confirmation copy moves information but doesn’t build the relationship. Wasted micro-interactions erode trust slowly and invisibly.

Before
Your account has been successfully updated.
After
You’re all set. We’ll let you know if anything needs your attention.
The original confirms a system action. The rewrite confirms an emotional state — and removes the burden of follow-up from the user entirely. Clearer copy, yes. Also a different relationship.

IV — Re-engagement email leading with the task

The message puts the burden back on the user before giving them a reason to care. Reminding them of the incomplete task reinforces the obligation without addressing the belief that created the hesitation.

Before
Complete your setup to start using [product].
After
Subject: You’re closer than you think. — You’re already set up. Most people start seeing results within minutes of logging back in. The only thing left is to take a look.
The original frames the situation as incomplete. The rewrite frames it as already resolved — and addresses the underlying belief before the user has a chance to act on it.

V — Health and wellness category reframe

Copy leads with the solution before resolving the customer’s existing belief. Competing on familiar terrain against ten products making the same claim.

Before
Boost your energy naturally with our scientifically formulated supplement.
After
You don’t have an energy problem. You have a processing problem.
Every other supplement is solving the same stated problem. This rewrite names a different problem — one the customer recognizes as more accurate. The category reframe is the entry point.
Section IV
Brand Voice
System

Most voice guides describe voice in adjectives. A real voice system answers three questions: What does this brand say? What does it never say? How does it sound when the stakes change?

Direct, not blunt.

Get to the point. Don’t pad. Don’t hedge. Avoid barking. Every sentence that says the same thing in half the words is a better sentence.

Certain without explaining itself.

A confident brand doesn’t justify its claims — it makes them and moves on. The moment you start defending, you’ve signaled doubt.

Specific over generic.

The copy that speaks to everyone speaks to no one. Specificity is what makes the customer feel seen.

Calibrated to the moment.

Brand voice isn’t one volume. Campaign headlines at full intensity, error states at a lower register. The same personality. Different rooms.

Earn the claim before you make it.

Lead with the specific fact. Let the reader draw the conclusion. They will — and they’ll trust it more because they got there themselves.

Write for the person who hasn’t decided yet.

Most copy is written for the customer who’s already convinced. The hard version — the one that actually drives conversion — is written for the one who’s still circling.

Section V
Applied Work
Real Stakes

The copy wasn’t in the job description. At every venue I managed, I took it on because it’s my strength and because leaving it wrong wasn’t an option when I was the one responsible for the room.

Rosemary & Beauty Queen

Nashville  ·  On-site General Manager  ·  rosemaryandbeautyqueen.com
The Role

On-site General Manager — operations, inventory, staff, and special events. Menu development and cocktail concepts weren’t a side project. A GM who understands the brand is the only one who can write a menu the room will actually sell.

The Copy Work

Original cocktail concepts and menu development. REMAKE/REMODEL. THREE & NINE. AVALON. Each name is a brief that fits in three words: what it tastes like, what it feels like, and what it says about where you are.

A menu written by a GM who runs the floor reads differently from one written by a copywriter who visited once. Every word has to be sellable by a server at 9pm on a Saturday.

IGC Hospitality

NYC & Orlando  ·  Floor Manager  ·  igchospitality.com
The Role

Floor manager, 30+ staff per night. Park Avenue Tavern. The Refinery Rooftop. The Wilson. Take Care. Winnie’s Jazz Bar. Managing at that scale means reading the room constantly and understanding what each concept owes its customer.

The Copy Work

Original cocktail concepts and menu development across the portfolio. A rooftop bar in Midtown and a jazz bar downtown don’t share a customer or a voice. Writing for both in the same week — and making neither sound like the other — is the practical definition of brand range.

Flamingo Cocktail Club

Nashville  ·  General Manager  ·  Full operational purview

Every venue I managed had copy that needed work. I didn’t wait for someone to ask. I rewrote the menus, developed the cocktail concepts, wrote the social copy, and made the brand say what the room actually was. The feedback loop that agency work never provides — whether the language landed, whether it sold — hospitality operations provides in real time, every shift.

Straps Leather

DTC brand  ·  Founded & launched  ·  strapsleather.com

Full brand launch from zero. Named the company. Defined the product line. Built the site. The leather pet accessories category runs between heritage craft brands and DTC lifestyle brands. Straps positions between them — direct and product-honest. One word. A noun that is also a product category.

A copywriter who has launched their own brand understands something a hired writer often doesn’t: every word costs something. When it’s your own brand, your own money, your own name on the door, you learn to make every decision count.

Section VI
Who
I Am

I’ve never worked inside an agency. Never had a creative director tell me what was acceptable, a billing structure that capped ambition, or a client approval process that sanded the edges off everything interesting. Every instinct I have was developed in the open — tested against real work, real audiences, real consequences, with no one to defer to and nowhere to hide.

The result is range. And a standard that comes from inside rather than from a hierarchy above. I don’t know how to do mediocre work on purpose. I don’t have the institutional training for it.

The background looks unconventional on paper. Politics before I knew it was brand strategy. Novels before I understood they were voice training. Copy work across more categories than most specialists ever touch, without the specialization ever narrowing what I could see. My mind doesn’t stop working a problem. It turns it over, comes back to it, finds the angle that wasn’t visible from the front.

The certifications I didn’t have, I went and got. HubSpot in SEO, Content Marketing, and Email Marketing. GA4. WordPress. Identified, scheduled, finished. The gaps the market kept surfacing — I closed them, because the standard I hold myself to required it, not because someone asked.

Writing
