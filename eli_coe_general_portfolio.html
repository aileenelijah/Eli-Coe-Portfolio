Most copy problems aren’t writing problems. They’re diagnosis problems. The wrong belief identified, the wrong friction addressed, the wrong person in the room when the brief was written. Fix the diagnosis and the writing follows. Skip it and you’re just making the same mistake sound better.

I grew up around politics — where every message is pressure-tested against the widest possible range of listeners before it earns the right to run. The line either means the same thing everywhere it lands, or it doesn’t work. Most brand copy fails that test. I build toward it.

Three novels. A screenplay that won at the Atlanta Comedy Film Festival. Years of copy across DTC, SaaS, and fintech. A childhood spent watching language move people toward decisions that shaped elections. The instincts were always there. This portfolio is what they produce when pointed at the right brief.

01  ·  Original Concepts

Four campaigns.
One point of view.

Each started with a specific brand problem, a specific audience, and a deliberate choice about the emotional territory no one else in the category was claiming.

Concept 01

The Wave.

For resilience-first consumer brands — health, wellness, financial recovery — that talk about strength but only ever show the triumphant after, never the difficult middle.

The Problem

Resilience advertising has a specific failure mode: it shows the breakthrough. It markets to the winner while ignoring the enormous, underserved audience still in it — not broken, not done, just still going. Underrepresented in every category that talks about strength, because those campaigns are built around the after.

The Campaign
You’ve been knocked down enough times
to stop counting.
Point at the next one.
Executions
Billboard

The wave is coming anyway.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Short

You stopped asking if you could handle it. You already know you can.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Medium

There’s a specific kind of person who has been through enough that fear stopped being the deciding factor. They don’t feel brave. They just stopped waiting to.  [Brand]

Video VO — 60s

You’ve been knocked down by enough of them to know what they feel like coming. The fear doesn’t go away. You just stop letting it decide. Point at the next one. [Brand]. For the ones still in it.

Concept 02

Runway.

For creator tools, AI products, and platforms built around the gap between the idea and the moment it actually gets made.

The Problem

Most ideas don’t make it. For lack of runway — not capability, not vision. The length of track between the idea and the moment it lifts off. The filmmaker who knew exactly what the shot was supposed to look like. The writer who had the story for three years. They didn’t need more talent. They needed somewhere for the idea to go.

The Campaign
You finally have the runway.
Make the thing you’ve been carrying.
Executions
Billboard

The idea was never the problem. You just didn’t have the runway.  [Brand]

Paid Social

The human brain is limited by time. Every idea you have is in competition with everything else — until something clears the path. You’ve been carrying this long enough.  [Brand]

OOH

The fist opens.  [Brand]

Video VO — 60s

You’ve had this idea for a long time. You knew exactly what it was supposed to look like. You just didn’t have the runway to make it real. The brain is limited by time. A fist slowly closing. You finally have the runway. Make the thing you’ve been carrying. [Brand]

Concept 03

Time.

For brands in health, financial services, and personal growth where survival mode is the real competitor — the thing consuming the customer’s time without their consent.

The Problem

Time is the only thing that doesn’t come back. The particular cost of being in survival mode is that it consumes time without consent. The brands that acknowledge this — that name the specific grief of time that slipped away — earn a kind of trust that brands who only talk about the future never reach.

The Campaign
You can’t get it back.
You can decide what you do with what’s left.
Executions
Billboard

Later keeps becoming now.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Short

You’re not running out of time. You’re running out of laters.  [Brand]

Paid Social — Medium

There’s a version of your life where you did the thing. Both versions start the same way. With a decision.  [Brand]

Video VO — 30s

You’ve been telling yourself later. Later has a way of becoming now without asking permission. You already know what you’ve been waiting to do. [Brand]

Concept 04

Do It Anyway.

For high-friction, high-commitment categories — financial products, health interventions, career change — where the customer has been circling the decision for months and hasn’t moved.

The Problem

Most brands in high-friction categories try to reduce perceived risk. “Easy. Simple. Get started in minutes.” Copy written for someone who just needed a small push. It doesn’t reach the person who has been thinking about this for a year, who doesn’t feel ready, who has failed before. Easier isn’t the answer. Permission to move without feeling ready is.

The Campaign
You’re not ready.
You never were.
Do it anyway.
Executions
Billboard

You’ve survived everything so far.  [Brand]

Paid Social

Nobody feels ready. The people who do the thing anyway just stopped waiting to.  [Brand]

Email Subject

You’ve been waiting to feel ready.

Video VO — 30s

You didn’t feel ready for any of this. You did it anyway. The only kind of ready that exists. [Brand]

04  ·  Diagnostic Work

The diagnosis comes
before the copy.

Copy that doesn’t work usually has a problem underneath the problem. These show the diagnosis before the fix — because if you don’t identify what’s actually wrong, the rewrite just covers it up.

01 — Feature-heavy product messaging

Copy describes what the product does without connecting to what the user actually wants. Positioned against competitors instead of against the user’s existing frustration.

Before
Our platform provides innovative solutions to streamline your workflow and enhance productivity outcomes.
After
You already know what’s slowing you down. We help you fix it — without adding three more tools to your stack.
The original speaks about the product. The rewrite speaks to the experience — and pre-empts the tool-fatigue objection in the same breath.

02 — Luxury brand over-explaining

Copy tries to justify the price point instead of assuming the customer already understands value. The moment you explain luxury, you’ve already lost the positioning battle.

Before
Our products are crafted using the highest quality ingredients, carefully sourced from around the world to deliver results you can see and feel.
After
If you know, you know.
The original is written for someone who needs convincing. The rewrite is written for someone who doesn’t. Restraint is the signal.

03 — Transactional lifecycle messaging

Confirmation copy moves information but doesn’t build the relationship. Every system message is a micro-interaction. Wasted micro-interactions erode trust slowly and invisibly.

Before
Your account has been successfully updated.
After
You’re all set. We’ll let you know if anything needs your attention.
The original confirms a system action. The rewrite confirms an emotional state — and removes the burden of follow-up from the user entirely. Clearer copy, yes. Also a different relationship.

04 — Re-engagement email leading with the task

The message puts the burden back on the user before they’ve been given a reason to care. Reminding them of the incomplete task reinforces obligation without addressing the belief that created the hesitation.

Before
Complete your setup to start using [product].
After
Subject: You’re closer than you think. — You’re already set up. Most people start seeing results within minutes of logging back in. The only thing left is to take a look.
The original frames the situation as incomplete. The rewrite frames it as already resolved — and addresses the underlying belief before the user has a chance to act on it.

05 — Health and wellness category reframe

Copy leads with the solution before resolving the customer’s existing belief. The brand is competing on familiar terrain against ten products making the same claim.

Before
Boost your energy naturally with our scientifically formulated supplement.
After
You don’t have an energy problem. You have a processing problem.
Every other energy supplement is solving the same stated problem. This rewrite names a different problem — one the customer recognizes as more accurate. The category reframe is the entry point.
05  ·  Brand Voice System

Building
what scales.

Most voice guides describe voice in adjectives — confident, warm, approachable, bold — without showing what those adjectives mean in practice. A real voice system answers three questions: What does this brand say? What does it never say? How does it sound when the stakes change?

Direct, not blunt.

Get to the point. Don’t pad. Don’t hedge. Avoid barking. There’s a version of every sentence that says the same thing in half the words without sounding curt.

Certain without explaining itself.

A confident brand doesn’t justify its claims — it makes them and moves on. The moment you start defending, you’ve signaled doubt.

Specific over generic.

The copy that speaks to everyone speaks to no one. Specificity is what makes the customer feel seen.

Calibrated to the moment.

Brand voice isn’t one volume. It’s a range — campaign headlines at full intensity, error states at a lower register. The same personality. Different rooms.

Earn the claim before you make it.

Lead with the specific fact. Let the reader draw the conclusion. They will — and they’ll trust it more because they got there themselves.

06  ·  Applied Work — Real Brands, Real Stakes

The copy wasn’t
in the job description.
I did it anyway.

At every venue I managed, the copy fell to me — because I saw where it was weak and knew I could fix it — the copy fell to me, not by assignment. Running operations and owning the brand voice simultaneously produces something a traditional copy background doesn’t: the knowledge of whether the language actually works. Every shift was a live test. Every weekend was a verdict.

Rosemary & Beauty Queen

Nashville · On-site General Manager · rosemaryandbeautyqueen.com
The Role

On-site General Manager — operations, inventory, staff, and special events. Menu development and cocktail concepts were part of running the place. A GM who understands the brand is the only one who can write a menu the room will actually sell.

The Copy Work

Original cocktail concepts and menu development. REMAKE/REMODEL. THREE & NINE. AVALON. Each name is a brief that fits in three words: what it tastes like, what it feels like, and what it says about where you are. Written by someone who had just finished the inventory count and had a reservation sheet to manage.

A menu written by a GM who runs the floor reads differently from one written by a copywriter who visited once. Every word has to be sellable by a server at 9pm on a Saturday.

IGC Hospitality

NYC & Orlando · Floor Manager, high-volume operations · igchospitality.com
The Role

Floor manager, 30+ staff per night across multiple concepts — Park Avenue Tavern, The Refinery Rooftop, The Wilson, Take Care, Winnie’s Jazz Bar. Managing at that scale means reading the room constantly and understanding what each concept owes its customer.

The Copy Work

Original cocktail concepts and menu development across the portfolio. A rooftop bar in Midtown and a jazz bar downtown don’t share a customer or a voice. Writing for both in the same week — and making neither sound like the other — is the practical definition of brand range.

Flamingo Cocktail Club

Nashville · General Manager — full operational purview

Every venue I managed had copy that needed work. I didn’t wait for someone to ask. I rewrote the menus, developed the cocktail concepts, wrote the social copy, and made the brand say what the room actually was. The feedback loop that agency work never provides — whether the language landed, whether it sold — hospitality operations provides in real time, every shift.

Straps Leather

DTC leather brand · Founded & launched · strapsleather.com

Full brand launch from zero. Named the company. Defined the product line. Built the site on Webflow. The leather pet accessories category runs between heritage craft brands and DTC lifestyle brands. Straps positions between them — direct and product-honest. One word. A noun that is also a product category.

A copywriter who has launched their own brand understands something a hired writer often doesn’t: every word costs something. When it’s your own brand, your own money, your own name on the door, you learn to make every decision count.

I’ve never worked inside an agency. Every instinct I have was developed in the open — tested against real work, real audiences, real consequences. The instincts were always there. This portfolio is what they produce when pointed at the right brief.

I’ve been the hardest boss I’ve ever had. I’m not done proving it.
